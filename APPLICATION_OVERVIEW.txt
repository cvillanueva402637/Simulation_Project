1. PROBLEM STATEMENT
================================================================================

Every year, thousands of transplantable kidneys are discarded in the United
States — not because recipients do not exist, but because the logistics of
matching, testing, transporting, and scheduling surgery cannot keep pace with
the narrow biological window in which a kidney remains viable.

  THE SCALE OF THE CRISIS
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  As of 2023, the nonuse (discard) rate for deceased donor kidneys in the U.S.
  reached 27.9% — up from 26.6% in 2022 and from roughly 18–20% just a decade
  ago (Lentine et al., 2025). Each year, approximately 3,500 to 3,800 donated
  kidneys are discarded (McKenney et al., 2024; Mohan et al., 2018), while
  more than 100,000 patients remain on the national transplant waitlist.

  THE ROOT CAUSES
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Research shows that discard reasons extend well beyond simple organ quality:

    • No Recipient Located in Time  — Allocation and matching delays account
      for up to 60% of discard reasons in some analyses (McKenney et al., 2024).
      When a compatible recipient cannot be identified and prepared before cold
      ischemia time expires, a viable organ is wasted.

    • Cold Ischemia Time Accumulation  — The longer a kidney remains on ice,
      the greater the risk of functional decline and discard. Each hour spent
      in matching queues, awaiting lab results, or waiting for a transport
      vehicle or operating room compounds this risk (Friedewald et al., 2023).

    • Biopsy and Clinical Findings  — The most frequently recorded clinical
      reason for discard, accounting for ~38.2% of cases (Mohan et al., 2018).
      While some of these are unavoidable, the compounding effect of logistics
      delays increases the likelihood that borderline organs are ultimately
      rejected.

    • Donor Characteristics and High-Risk Organs  — Kidneys from older,
      obese, diabetic, or hypertensive donors have significantly higher discard
      odds. For organs with a Kidney Donor Profile Index (KDPI) >= 85%,
      non-utilization rates exceed 50–60% (Friedewald et al., 2023;
      Lentine et al., 2025).

  THE OPPORTUNITY FOR SIMULATION
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Because a substantial share of discards is attributable to logistical and
  allocation inefficiencies — not irreversible organ failure — there is a
  meaningful opportunity to reduce waste through better resource planning
  and process design. However, the transplant pipeline is a complex stochastic
  system: arrival rates are random, matching is probabilistic, resources are
  shared and finite, and the cost of failure is a human life.

  This is precisely the class of problem that discrete-event simulation is
  designed to address.

  This application models the full kidney transplant logistics chain as a
  SimPy-based Arena-equivalent DES, enabling researchers and planners to:

    1. Quantify how resource bottlenecks (labs, transport, ORs) translate
       directly into cold ischemia time accumulation and organ expiry.
    2. Test the sensitivity of organ waste and waitlist mortality to changes
       in resource capacity, arrival rates, and matching thresholds.
    3. Identify which stages of the pipeline — allocation, testing, transport,
       or surgery — are the primary drivers of preventable discard.
    4. Produce statistically rigorous outputs (confidence intervals across
       10 replications) to support evidence-based policy recommendations.

  REFERENCES
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Friedewald, J. J., et al. (2023). Kidney donor profile index and discard
    rates for high-KDPI organs. American Journal of Transplantation.

  Lentine, K. L., et al. (2025). OPTN/SRTR 2023 Annual Data Report: Kidney.
    American Journal of Transplantation, 25(S1).

  McKenney, A. S., et al. (2024). Trends and reasons for deceased donor kidney
    discard in the United States. Clinical Journal of the American Society of
    Nephrology.

  Mohan, S., et al. (2018). Factors leading to the discard of deceased donor
    kidneys in the United States. Kidney International, 94(1), 187–197.


2. OBJECTIVES OF THE APPLICATION
================================================================================

The application is an Arena-style Discrete-Event Simulation (DES) of a kidney
transplant supply chain, built in Python using SimPy and wrapped in a Tkinter
GUI that mimics the look and feel of Rockwell Arena Simulation Software.

Core objectives:

  a) MODEL ORGAN PERISHABILITY
     Every kidney has a hard 36-hour cold ischemia deadline. Quality decays
     continuously using Q(t) = e^(-0.05*t). The simulation measures how many
     organs expire before ever reaching a recipient.

  b) REPLICATE UNOS-STYLE ORGAN ALLOCATION
     Organs are matched to the highest-priority compatible patient using ABO
     blood-type compatibility, 6-antigen HLA scoring, PRA (Panel Reactive
     Antibody) screening, and a composite urgency/wait-time score.

  c) MODEL RESOURCE CONTENTION
     Four shared resources (Operating Rooms, Surgical Teams, Transport
     Vehicles, Crossmatch Labs) create realistic bottlenecks and queues.

  d) QUANTIFY WAITLIST OUTCOMES
     Track how many patients are transplanted, die waiting, or are transferred
     — providing a systemic view of supply/demand imbalance.

  e) ENABLE "WHAT-IF" EXPERIMENTATION
     The Run Setup dialog lets you adjust resource capacities, arrival rates,
     and perishability parameters so stakeholders can test policy changes
     (e.g., "What happens if we add a 4th transport vehicle?").

  f) PROVIDE STATISTICAL RIGOR
     10 independent replications with different random seeds generate
     confidence intervals on all output metrics, equivalent to Arena's
     replication analysis.

  g) SERVE AS AN ACADEMIC ARENA EQUIVALENT
     The entire model is structured around Arena's module types (Create,
     Assign, Decide, Process, Hold, Dispose) so it can be used to teach or
     demonstrate DES concepts.

4. LIMITATIONS — LINKED TO THE GUI
================================================================================

  a) SINGLE TRANSPLANT CENTER
     The model simulates one facility only, not a regional/national UNOS
     network. No inter-center organ sharing or geographic routing beyond
     abstract Local/Regional/National transport zones.
     → GUI: Setup dialog — transport zone is stochastic, not a real map.

  b) NO PEDIATRIC VS. ADULT SEGMENTATION
     Patients are Uniform(18–75) age with no age-based priority or pediatric
     allocation rules. Misses UNOS pediatric priority rules.
     → GUI: No age filter in the Run Setup.

  c) SIMPLIFIED PRA MODEL
     PRA affects crossmatch failure probability probabilistically, not via
     actual antibody-antigen specificity. Over-simplifies highly sensitized
     patient outcomes.
     → GUI: Reports tab — crossmatch failure rate is aggregated, not
       patient-specific.

  d) FIXED RESOURCE CAPACITIES DURING A RUN
     ORs, teams, vehicles, and labs cannot be changed mid-simulation. Cannot
     model shift schedules, overtime, or resource breakdowns.
     → GUI: Setup dialog parameters are locked once Run is pressed.

  e) NO ORGAN RE-OFFERING LOGIC
     If a crossmatch fails or transport expires, the organ is disposed — not
     re-offered to the next compatible patient. Overestimates organ waste
     compared to real UNOS where organs are re-listed.
     → GUI: "No Compatible Match" and "Organs Expired" counters will read
       higher than reality.

  f) HOMOGENEOUS PATIENT POPULATION
     No diabetes, hypertension, or comorbidity modeling. Success probabilities
     don't account for recipient health status beyond HLA/PRA.
     → GUI: Transplant success formula is HLA + CIT only (no comorbidity
       input field in Setup).

  g) NO FINANCIAL / COST MODELING
     No cost per OR-hour, transport cost, or insurance variables. Cannot
     perform cost-effectiveness or health-economics analysis.
     → GUI: No cost output anywhere in Reports or Plots tabs.

  h) WARM-UP PERIOD EDGE CASE
     Warm-up is fixed at 30 days. If Duration is set very short (e.g., 7
     days) via the toolbar spinbox, the warm-up consumes the entire run and
     statistics will be empty or misleading.
     → GUI: Status bar clock — run ends with no post-warmup data collected.

  i) DISPLAY-ONLY PAUSE
     The Pause button stops the GUI update thread, not the underlying SimPy
     engine. Pausing doesn't freeze simulation time; it just delays screen
     refresh.
     → GUI: Toolbar — Pause button is a display-only hold.

  j) DETERMINISTIC QUALITY DECAY
     Q(t) = e^(-0.05*t) is the same for all organ types. No distinction
     between kidneys from older vs. younger donors. Understates real
     variability in organ quality.
     → GUI: Plots tab → Cold Ischemia / Quality chart shows a single curve.

  ── SLIDE 1 — PROJECT TITLE ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Kidney Transplant Logistics & Perishable Queueing Simulation
  Arena-Style Discrete-Event Simulation | Python + SimPy | 2025

  ── SLIDE 2 — PROBLEM STATEMENT ──────────────────────────────────────────────
  • 100,000+ patients are on the US kidney transplant waitlist at any time
  • As of 2023, 27.9% of all recovered deceased donor kidneys were discarded
    — up from 26.6% in 2022 and from ~18-20% just a decade ago
    (Lentine et al., 2025)
  • ~3,500–3,800 donated kidneys are discarded annually in the U.S.
    (McKenney et al., 2024; Mohan et al., 2018)
  • Top discard reasons: No recipient located in time (up to 60% of cases),
    biopsy findings (38.2%), prolonged cold ischemia, and donor characteristics
    — many of which are addressable through better logistics (McKenney et al.,
    2024; Mohan et al., 2018)
  • Goal: Simulate the end-to-end transplant pipeline to identify where
    allocation friction and resource bottlenecks drive preventable organ waste
    and patient deaths

  ── SLIDE 3 — SYSTEM OVERVIEW ────────────────────────────────────────────────
  • Two entity types: Donor kidneys (~3/day) and Waitlist patients (~6/day)
  • Three process stages: Crossmatch Lab → Transport → Surgery
  • Four shared resources: 2 Labs, 3 Transport Vehicles, 4 ORs, 5 Teams
  • Allocation logic: UNOS-style ABO + HLA + PRA + priority score matching
  • Simulation horizon: 1 year | 10 replications | 30-day warm-up

  ── SLIDE 4 — KEY MODEL FEATURES ─────────────────────────────────────────────
  Feature              | Detail
  ---------------------|----------------------------------------------------
  Perishability        | Cold ischemia clock, Q(t) = e^(-0.05*t) decay
  UNOS Allocation      | Blood type + HLA + PRA + urgency/wait-time score
  Attrition            | Patients die (0.0001/hr) or transfer (0.00005/hr)
  Living Donors        | Bypass matching; go directly to crossmatch & OR
  Statistics           | Tally, Time-Persistent, Counter — 95% CIs across
                       | replications

  ── SLIDE 5 — GUI WALKTHROUGH ────────────────────────────────────────────────
  • Toolbar           — Run, Reset, Setup; set Replications, Duration, Seed
  • Flowchart Canvas  — Visual Arena-style process map (3 entity paths)
  • Live Stats Sidebar — Real-time counters and resource utilization
  • Reports Tab       — Arena-style tabular output with confidence intervals
  • Plots Tab         — Dashboard, queue time-series, utilization, outcomes

  ── SLIDE 6 — KEY LIMITATIONS ────────────────────────────────────────────────
  • Single-center model (no UNOS network)
  • No organ re-offering after crossmatch failure
  • No comorbidity / recipient health modeling
  • Resource capacities fixed for duration of run
  • No financial / cost analysis

  ── SLIDE 7 — WHAT CAN BE TESTED ─────────────────────────────────────────────
  • Add a 4th transport vehicle    → Does organ waste drop?
  • Increase arrival rate          → Does the waitlist grow unbounded?
  • Reduce cold ischemia limit     → How sensitive is the system to tighter
                                     viability windows?
  • Double OR capacity             → Does surgery become the bottleneck or
                                     does transport?
